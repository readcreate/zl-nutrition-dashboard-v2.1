Situational Analysis Framework (SAF)

Key Domains

  • Service availability (which services, at which level, what hours)
  • Infrastructure readiness (space, water, electricity, sanitation)
  • Human resources (staffing, cadres, training, competency)
  • Commodities & medicines (oxytocin, MgSO4, contraceptives, vaccines)
  • Clinical guidelines & protocols availability
  • HMIS data quality & record-keeping
  • Quality of care (direct observation, record review)
  • Demand & utilization (exit interviews, community surveys)

Data Collection Methods

  • Structured facility checklists & inventories
  • Health worker interviews & competency assessments
  • Client exit interviews
  • HMIS data review
  • Direct observation of service delivery

Strengths for PIH

  • Highly standardized; tools can be harmonized across all PIH countries
  • Validated instruments exist (DHS Service Provision Assessment tools)
  • Produces quantitative outputs aggregable across sites
  • Familiar to Ministries of Health — facilitates buy-in
  • Can be led by trained implementation committee members

Limitations

  • Primarily facility-focused; underrepresents community dynamics
  • Requires moderate-to-high HMIS data availability
  • Resource-intensive if applied across many facilities
  • Snapshot in time; misses seasonal/cyclical variation

Typical Outputs

  • Facility readiness scorecards by service area
  • Gap analysis tables by domain and site
  • SARA-style service availability & readiness indices
  • Prioritized infrastructure, HR, and commodity gap lists

WHO Health Systems Building Blocks

The Six Building Blocks

  • 1. Service Delivery — continuum of care coverage, quality, equity
  • 2. Health Workforce — density, skill mix, training, retention
  • 3. Health Information Systems — vital registration, HMIS quality, data use
  • 4. Essential Medicines & Technologies — supply chain, cold chain, procurement
  • 5. Health Financing — expenditure, OOP burden, financial protection
  • 6. Leadership & Governance — policy, accountability, partner coordination

Strengths for PIH

  • Comprehensive systems view beyond facility readiness
  • Universally applicable across PIH country contexts (Rwanda, Haiti, SL, Malawi, Liberia, and others)
  • Aligned with MoH planning cycles; findings directly relevant to governments
  • Identifies root causes rather than symptoms
  • Linked to global reporting (SDGs, UHC index, GFF)

Limitations

  • Broad scope can feel diffuse without RMNCAH-specific indicators
  • Requires high-level data (national health accounts, workforce census)
  • Governance & financing blocks need significant technical expertise
  • May be too macro for site-level committees without strong facilitation
  • Risk of findings too systemic for actionable program recommendations

Bottleneck Analysis (BNA) / Tanahashi Model

The 6 Determinants of Coverage

  • 1. Commodity availability — medicines, supplies, vaccines at point of care
  • 2. HR availability / geographic access — trained workers present, facility density
  • 3. Accessibility — geographic, financial, temporal access
  • 4. Initial utilization / acceptability — social norms, trust, male partner dynamics
  • 5. Continuous utilization — completing ANC visits, vaccination schedules
  • 6. Effective coverage — service quality sufficient to produce health outcomes

Strengths for PIH

  • Actionable by design: each bottleneck implies specific interventions
  • Continuum-of-care maps perfectly onto RMNCAH program logic
  • Strong equity lens: disaggregates by wealth, geography, gender
  • Uses routine HMIS data as starting point, reducing primary collection burden
  • Widely used by UNICEF/WHO in all PIH countries
  • Communicable to non-technical audiences

Limitations

  • Data quality dependency: weak HMIS (Haiti, Sierra Leone) undermines estimates
  • Oversimplifies complex causal pathways; bottlenecks interact
  • Demand-side often underdeveloped in standard BNA applications
  • Requires skilled facilitation to move from bottleneck to root cause

Rapid Health Facility Assessment (RHFA)

Assessment Domains

  • Infrastructure & utilities (ANC/delivery rooms, water, electricity, sanitation)
  • Essential equipment (delivery beds, neonatal resuscitation, BP cuffs, cold chain)
  • Essential medicines (oxytocin, MgSO4, misoprostol, antibiotics, vaccines)
  • Human resources (cadres present, RMNCAH training status)
  • Guidelines, protocols & job aids
  • Service statistics (ANC visits, deliveries, immunizations)
  • Optional: direct observation of clinical encounter

Strengths for PIH

  • 2–4 hours per facility: feasible for site committees with limited time
  • Same checklist across Rwanda, Haiti, Sierra Leone, Malawi, Liberia — comparable scores
  • Generates readiness index trackable over time (ideal for monitoring progress)
  • Can be used by non-specialist program staff and clinical supervisors
  • CommCare / KoboToolbox compatible for real-time aggregation
  • Ideal for periodic reassessment (quarterly or biannual)

Limitations

  • Captures inputs/processes only — not outcomes or quality of care
  • Snapshot: stock status on assessment day ≠ typical day
  • No community voice or health-seeking behavior insight
  • Requires structured follow-up — scores useless without action planning
  • No governance, financing, or policy dimension

SWOT-Based Participatory Landscape Assessment

The Four Analytical Dimensions

  • Strengths — existing assets: CHW networks, community trust, MoH partnership
  • Weaknesses — internal gaps: staff turnover, weak data, fragmented service delivery
  • Opportunities — external leverage: GFF funding, mHealth platforms, civil society advocacy
  • Threats — external risks: political instability, economic shocks, climate disruptions, brain drain

Methods Used

  • Focus group discussions (women, mothers, adolescent girls, male partners)
  • Key informant interviews (facility managers, district officers, MoH)
  • Community dialogues and town halls
  • Participatory geographic mapping
  • Most Significant Change storytelling
  • Stakeholder validation workshops

Strengths for PIH

  • Aligns with PIH's community-centered model and accompaniment philosophy
  • Surfaces social norms, power dynamics, community trust — invisible to checklists
  • Low data dependency: works even in Haiti and Sierra Leone
  • Builds local ownership and implementation committee capacity
  • Flexible and culturally adaptable across PIH country contexts

Limitations

  • Subjectivity risk if facilitation is not skilled and structured
  • Not quantitative: no indicators or readiness scores for funders
  • Time-intensive: thorough participatory collection takes longer than checklists
  • Synthesis challenge: rich qualitative data → actionable priorities requires capacity
  • May be perceived as less rigorous by technical steering committee members

PIH 5-S Integrative Framework (Mary's Proposal)

The 5 Ss — Proposed Structure

  • Staff — workforce availability, training, supervision, retention
  • Stuff — medicines, commodities, equipment, supply chain
  • Space — physical infrastructure, utilities, geographic access
  • Support Structure — policies, governance, data systems, financing
  • 5th S (TBD) — Sociocultural context? Systems? Community voice?

How It Draws From the 5 Models

  • Staff → SAF (HR domain), RHFA (HR checklist), BNA (HR bottleneck)
  • Stuff → SAF, RHFA, BNA (commodity availability)
  • Space → RHFA (infrastructure checklist), SAF (service availability)
  • Support Structure → WHO Building Blocks (governance, financing, HIS)
  • 5th S → SWOT Participatory (community voice, context)

Potential Advantages

  • PIH-owned framework: steering committee controls its scope and depth
  • Intuitive language for community members and non-technical partners
  • Can cherry-pick validated instruments from each of the 5 models
  • Avoids redundancy by mapping domains across models
  • Flexible: add/remove modules by site context

Considerations / Risks

  • Requires upfront synthesis work by the steering committee
  • Not externally validated — may require additional justification for funders
  • 5th S is still undefined — needs committee decision
  • Risk of over-inclusion: "pulling from all 5" can create a very long tool
  • Need to decide: baseline only, or trackable over time?

5th S — Selected Option

Pathway Options for the Steering Committee

Three approaches for committee consideration, from most comprehensive to most rapid. Sites currently in scope: Rwanda (3 teams), Haiti (1 team), Sierra Leone — Malawi and Liberia TBD. Lesotho not formally included but part of the conversation.

A
Recommended · Hybrid Approach
PIH 5-S + RHFA Backbone + SWOT 5th S
Use Mary's 5-S framework as the organizing structure. Populate Staff, Stuff, and Space using RHFA instruments (fast, standardized, CommCare-compatible). Use WHO Building Block domains for Support Structure. Add a participatory SWOT module for the 5th S to capture community voice and contextual factors. Pull BNA bottleneck analysis for priority interventions identified in phase 2.
Components RHFA checklist (Staff + Stuff + Space) · WHO governance/HIS/financing domains (Support Structure) · Participatory FGDs + community mapping (5th S Sociocultural) · BNA waterfall for gap prioritization
B
Option · Rapid Baseline
RHFA + Targeted BNA
If time and resources are constrained, lead with RHFA for a rapid, quantitative baseline across confirmed sites. Layer a targeted BNA using existing HMIS/DHS data to identify the top 3 bottlenecks per site. Reserve deeper qualitative or participatory work for a Phase 2 gap assessment. Fastest path to a cross-site dashboard.
Components RHFA checklist at each site · BNA using routine data · Cross-site readiness scorecard · Bottleneck priority matrix
C
Option · Comprehensive Audit
SAF + WHO Building Blocks + SWOT
The most complete assessment: SAF for full facility and community readiness, WHO Building Blocks for governance/financing/policy analysis, and SWOT for community and stakeholder context. Produces the richest evidence base for a multi-year program plan. Most demanding in time, data, and facilitation expertise — best suited if the steering committee has 6–12 months and external technical support.
Components Full SAF facility + community survey · WHO Building Blocks key informant interviews · Participatory SWOT per site · Integrated gap analysis report
D
Option · Phased Approach
RHFA Now → SAF/BNA Later
Phase 1 (months 1–3): Deploy RHFA across all sites to establish a quantitative baseline with minimal burden. Use findings to determine which sites and domains need deeper investigation. Phase 2 (months 4–9): Apply SAF or BNA selectively to the highest-need sites or most critical gaps identified in Phase 1. Allows the committee to adapt the assessment depth to what is found.
Components Phase 1: RHFA confirmed sites · Phase 2: SAF or BNA for priority sites · Adaptive scope based on Phase 1 findings
