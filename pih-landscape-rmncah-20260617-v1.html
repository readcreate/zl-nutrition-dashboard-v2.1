Option 1 of 4 · Partners in Health · RMNCAH Landscape Assessment

WHO Building Blocks backbone

Evaluates how strong the RMNCAH system is in each country. Unit of analysis: the health system. WHOBB's six building blocks map onto the 5 Ss, with RHFA grounding Space and SWOT capturing Social Support.

Best for: donor-quality, cross-country system comparison.
Source Key WHO WHO Health Systems Building Blocks RHFA Rapid Health Facility Assessment (SARA) SWOT SWOT-Based Participatory Assessment
Why this structure
WHOBB is the backbone because it most strongly evaluates Systems and lends itself to cross-country comparison. Facility readiness (RHFA/SARA) grounds Space in concrete numbers, and SWOT brings in Social Support through participatory methods — the one dimension WHOBB cannot reach on its own.
Strengths
  • National-level systems assessment
  • Facility-level validation via RHFA
  • Strong cross-country comparability
Limitations
  • May not explain why outcomes are poor despite adequate system capacity
  • Requires technical expertise for governance/financing
  • Findings are macro — harder to action at program level
  • Requires high-level data (national health accounts, census) not always available site-level
Best for: a donor-quality landscape assessment that compares countries consistently.

Option 2 of 4 · Partners in Health · RMNCAH Landscape Assessment

SAF backbone

Answers what the RMNCAH situation is today and what's driving it. Unit of analysis: the RMNCAH service environment. Strong in translating findings directly into intervention.

Best for: program planning and investment decisions grounded in current-state data.
Source Key SAF Situational Analysis Framework WHO WHO Building Blocks (minimal — governance & financing only) SWOT SWOT-Based Participatory Assessment
Why this structure
SAF assesses both whether capacities exist and how available they are in practice — covering service availability, infrastructure readiness, human resources, commodities, protocols, data quality, and demand and utilization in a single pass. A minimal WHOBB layer covers governance and financing only, since SAF alone can't reach those domains. SWOT brings in Social Support.
Strengths
  • Strong translation to intervention
  • Covers service availability, infrastructure, HR, commodities, protocols, data, quality, and demand in one pass
Limitations
  • More of a "snapshot" of current state
  • Resource-intensive; requires high data availability
  • Requires technical expertise to assess governance and financing
Best for: understanding the current RMNCAH situation and what's driving it. Probably the strongest RMNCAH-specific option.

Option 3 of 4 · Partners in Health · RMNCAH Landscape Assessment

WHO Building Blocks alone

A clean, single-framework option. No supplements from SAF, BNA, or SWOT — this is WHOBB's six building blocks applied directly to the 5 Ss, showing what you get (and what's missing) without any hybrid layering.

Best for: teams prioritizing simplicity and standardization, where thinner coverage of Space and Social Support is an acceptable tradeoff.
Source Key WHO WHO Health Systems Building Blocks — the only source used in this option
Why this structure
Presented as a "clean" comparison point: a single, internationally standardized framework with no hybrid layering. WHOBB's six blocks (service delivery, workforce, HIS, medicines/technologies, financing, governance) already map reasonably well to 4 of the 5 Ss — but Space and Social Support are visibly thinner here than in the hybrid options, since WHOBB was never designed to assess physical facility conditions or community voice in detail.
Strengths
  • Single framework — easiest to train teams on and explain to MoH
  • Globally standardized; strong cross-country comparability
  • No need to coordinate multiple tools or methodologies
Limitations
  • Space coverage is shallow — no facility-level physical detail
  • Social Support is essentially a gap — community voice is a footnote, not a method
  • Requires technical expertise for governance/financing analysis
Best for: simplicity and standardization, if the team accepts thinner coverage of Space and Social Support.

Option 4 of 4 · Partners in Health · RMNCAH Landscape Assessment

SWOT alone

A clean, single-framework option using only participatory methods — interviews, focus groups, workshops. No supplements from WHOBB, SAF, or BNA. Presented to show what's gained in engagement and what's lost in standardization and rigor.

Best for: strategic planning and partnership development where stakeholder engagement and contextual nuance are the top priority.
Source Key SWOT SWOT-Based Participatory Assessment — the only source used in this option
Why this structure
A single-framework option built entirely on participatory methods — interviews, focus groups, and workshops. SWOT relies principally on qualitative data, and how thoroughly it touches each of the 5 Ss depends on who is consulted and what surfaces in conversation. Comparing findings across countries or facilities is harder without a standardized way to quantify them, and an identified weakness may not always point to its underlying cause.
Strengths
  • Highly engaging for country teams, ministries, and partners
  • Captures political realities, stakeholder priorities, and implementation feasibility
  • Strongest possible coverage of Social Support specifically
Limitations
  • Less standardized across countries — hard to compare apples to apples
  • Outcomes classified via group decision-making vary depending on who is in the room
  • May identify a weakness without identifying its underlying cause
  • Weak quantitative grounding for Staff, Stuff, Space, and Systems
Best for: strategic planning and partnership development, where capturing political realities and stakeholder priorities matters more than standardized, quantifiable comparison.
